But, we want to talk about *all* possible arrangements and *all* possible solutions. So it makes sense to forget about the actual contents of the blocks and just focus on what the rotations are actually doing.

The key insight is that a rotation is simply changing the *positions* of some of the blocks, and that a more complex sequence of rotations is doing the same thing. So keeping track of positions is likely to be helpful. We can represent the starting position simply as

It's going to get tiresome to keep saying things like "rotate the upper left corner". So let's agree that A stands for that, and B, C, and D stand for rotating the upper right, lower left, and lower right corners. It's also useful to note that doing A four times in a row does nothing -- or differently put, doing it three times in a row undoes it*{{If we wanted to get technical, we'd call it the *inverse*}}. Now try out the sequence ADad. It does something interesting -- it leaves the first and third columns alone, and cycles the middle column down one space (with the 8 floating back to the top). Naturally enough, this is called a 3-cycle. Sequences of moves like this, that make different and structured changes in a puzzle are often a key to its solution. If you've ever done Rubik's cube at all seriously you know this already!

# Some symmetry

Imagine picking up the puzzle and giving it a quarter-turn clockwise before beginning and then applying the moves ADad -- in the sense of "upper left, lower right, upper right backwards, lower right backwards" but using "upper right" in the new position. You'll still cycle three elements but it will be the tree elements 4, 5 and 6 rather than 2, 5 and 8. If instead of rotating the puzzle you'd flipped it over (did I tell you it was two-sided?) it would still be the positions 2, 5 and 8 that you changed, but you'd actually cycle them in the reverse order.

This is the basis for a general rule: any symmetric version of a transformation that we can achieve, is also achievable.
