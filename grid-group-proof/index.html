Imagine a puzzle — it consists of a 4×4 grid of coloured blocks that contain letters. It has little knobs that allow you to rotate any 2×2 group of blocks.

Your objective is to rearrange the blocks in such a way that each group of blocks of the same colour is connected and forms a word. This example is pretty easy! If you'd like to try some harder puzzles of this type before continuing, I suggest Wordtangle.

History

Puzzles built from shuffling blocks around a grid go back further than you might guess. The most famous ancestor is the 15 Puzzle, which swept the United States and then Europe in a matter of months in 1880. Its popular origin story is wrong, as it happens: the showman Sam Loyd spent twenty years claiming he'd invented it, but puzzle historians Jerry Slocum and Dic Sonneveld eventually traced the real inventor to Noyes Chapman, a postmaster in Canastota, New York, who'd been circulating homemade copies since 1874.

The 15 Puzzle slides tiles into a single empty gap. This puzzle instead rotates whole 2×2 blocks with no gap at all — but the two families are closer than they look: sliding a piece all the way around a loop back to where it started has exactly the same effect as a rotation. You can try both mechanics yourself in Simon Tatham's Portable Puzzle Collection: Fifteen is the classic sliding original, Sixteen shifts whole rows and columns instead, and Twiddle rotates 2×2 (or larger) blocks, just like here — Tatham says he first met that move inside a puzzle from Metroid Prime 2. Turning blocks is, of course, also basically what a Rubik's Cube does, just on the faces of a cube instead of a flat grid.

The mathematical version of this rotate-a-block puzzle was formalised recently, in Thomas Lam's 2022 paper "Solution to the Number Rotation Puzzle".

Another way, sometimes more compact, is to follow "chains of positions" — since 1 goes to 3, where does 3 go (to 6), then where does 6 go? Names and notation

It's going to get tiresome to keep saying things like "rotate the upper left corner". So let's agree that A stands for that, and B, C, and D stand for rotating the upper right, lower left, and lower right corners. It's also useful to note that doing A four times in a row does nothing — or differently put, doing it three times in a row undoes it*. That's so convenient it's worth having its own symbol, say a, and likewise b, c, and d.

We can write these down in terms of their loops:

Operation Cycles Inverse Cycles
A (1 2 5 4) a (1 4 5 2)
B (2 3 6 5) b (2 5 6 3)
C (4 5 8 7) c (4 7 8 5)
D (5 6 9 8) d (5 8 9 6)

And, we can see what happens when we apply them one after the other.

Now try out the sequence A D a d. It does something interesting — it leaves the first and third columns alone, and cycles the middle column down one space (with the 8 floating back to the top). Naturally enough, this is called a 3-cycle. Sequences of moves like this, that make different and structured changes in a puzzle, are often a key to its solution. If you've ever done Rubik's cube at all seriously you know this already!

Some symmetry

Imagine picking up the puzzle and giving it a quarter-turn clockwise before you start, and then applying the same moves A D a d as before, using the same corner locations as always. If you don't account for that initial turn, what you'll see is a messier tangle — the turn's own effect gets mixed in with the three-cycle. Rotate (or flip) the board below, apply A D a d, and then use "Restore orientation" to peel the initial turn back off: you'll find it's the three elements 4, 5 and 6 that cycled, rather than 2, 5 and 8. Flipping the board over instead (did I tell you it was two-sided?) leaves the positions 2, 5 and 8 unchanged as a set, but reverses the order in which they cycle.

This is the basis for a general rule: any symmetric version of a transformation that we can achieve, is also achievable.
