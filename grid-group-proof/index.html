Corner Cases

Which block-rotation puzzles are unconstrained (and why)?

Imagine a puzzle — it consists of a 4×4 grid of coloured blocks that contain letters. It has little knobs that allow you to rotate any 2×2 group of blocks.

Your objective is to rearrange the blocks in such a way that each group of blocks of the same colour is connected and forms a word. This example is pretty easy. If you'd like to try some harder puzzles of this type before continuing, I suggest Wordtangle.

History

Puzzles built from shuffling blocks around a grid go back further than you might guess. The most famous ancestor is the 15 Puzzle, which swept the United States and then Europe in a matter of months in 1880. Its popular origin story is wrong, as it happens: the showman Sam Loyd spent twenty years claiming he'd invented it, but puzzle historians Jerry Slocum and Dic Sonneveld eventually traced the real inventor to Noyes Chapman, a postmaster in Canastota, New York, who'd been circulating homemade copies since 1874.

The 15 Puzzle slides tiles into a single empty gap. This puzzle instead rotates whole 2×2 blocks with no gap at all — but the two families are closer than they look: sliding a piece all the way around a loop back to where it started has exactly the same effect as a rotation. You can try both mechanics yourself in Simon Tatham's Portable Puzzle Collection: Fifteen is the classic sliding original, Sixteen shifts whole rows and columns instead, and Twiddle rotates 2×2 (or larger) blocks, just like here — Tatham says he first met that move inside a puzzle from Metroid Prime 2. Turning blocks is, of course, also basically what a Rubik's Cube does, but on the faces of a cube instead of a flat grid.

Thomas Lam formalised the mathematical version of this rotate-a-block puzzle in his 2022 paper "Solution to the Number Rotation Puzzle".

First steps

To start with we're going to look at the corresponding question for a 3×3 grid. Why 3×3? Three turns out to be a magic number: a lot of interesting structure in problems like this first shows up exactly when you move from two objects to three — with two things you can only swap them or leave them alone, but three is where real cycles, and real complexity, begin. It's also a smaller board to reason about, and when we're done we might understand enough to handle the 4×4 case as well*.

Another way, sometimes more compact, is to follow "chains of positions" — since 1 goes to 3, where does 3 go (to 6), then where does 6 go? Names and notation

It's going to get tiresome to keep saying things like "rotate the upper left corner". Let's agree that A stands for that, and B, C, and D stand for rotating the upper right, lower left, and lower right corners. It's also useful to note that doing A four times in a row does nothing — or differently put, doing it three times in a row undoes it*. That's so convenient it's worth having its own symbol, say a, and likewise b, c, and d.

We can write these down in terms of their loops:

Operation Cycles Inverse Cycles
A (1 2 5 4) a (1 4 5 2)
B (2 3 6 5) b (2 5 6 3)
C (4 5 8 7) c (4 7 8 5)
D (5 6 9 8) d (5 8 9 6)

What happens when we apply them one after the other?

Try out the sequence A D a d. It does something interesting — nothing happens to the first and third columns, and the middle column cycles down one space, with the 8 floating back to the top. This is called a three-cycle. Sequences of moves like this, that make different and structured changes in a puzzle, are often a key to its solution. If you've ever done Rubik's cube at all seriously you know this already!

Some symmetry

Imagine picking up the puzzle and giving it a quarter-turn clockwise before you start, and then applying the same moves A D a d as before, using the same corner locations as always. If you don't account for that initial turn, what you'll see is a messier tangle — the turn's own effect gets mixed in with the three-cycle. Rotate the board below, apply A D a d, and then use "Restore orientation" to peel the initial turn back off: you'll find it's the three elements 4, 5 and 6 that cycled, rather than 2, 5 and 8. Flipping the board over instead (did I tell you it was two-sided?) leaves the positions 2, 5 and 8 unchanged as a set, but reverses the order in which they cycle.

Try out some other sequences of operations, and then do the same ones after a change of orientation, finishing by restoring the original orientation. The cycle structure should always be the same as the original, but the actual positions affected may change*.

This is the basis for a general rule: whatever symmetric version of a move we want, we can do that too.

All the threes

An interesting thing about a three-cycle (x y z) is that if you apply it twice you get (x z y), and if you apply it three times of course you get back to "nothing happens". This means that if we can produce a three-cycle that moves three specific positions, then we also have every three-cycle that moves just those three positions (since x has to go to y or z, and that determines the third one).

So far we only have the three-cycles that move the middle row or column. Can we get more? Yes — this is a standard trick. Imagine we do some moves, then we do the A D a d sequence (cycling the middle column) and then we undo the moves we originally did (do them again in reverse order, swapping each of A/a, B/b, C/c and D/d for its partner). What will we have moved then? Well, try it out.

We get another three-cycle that moves exactly the positions that were moved into the central column by our initial sequence of moves. And so we have all the three-cycles there. And also on all the symmetric versions of "there" obtained by rotating and flipping the square.

Three's a crowd

Triples are nice, but it turns out that we can't achieve all permutations using just three-cycles (don't ask). But two-cycles (usually called transpositions), that just exchange two elements, are a different story. Imagine we could use all the transpositions.

In the puzzle below you can exchange any two tiles by clicking them. Try to rearrange the grid so that 1–9 are in the correct order.

There are any number of good strategies: for instance, switch 1 with whatever is in its place, then switch 2 with whatever is in its place, …

In the original puzzle, we'd like to move just two elements, but the basic operations we have (the corner rotations) move four, and we've concentrated on getting others that move three. It's time, I'm afraid, for a bit of abstract thinking — and first I want to think about the other direction: suppose that I knew how to move triples and pairs, how could I move four elements using just one pair? Imagine we have four elements w, x, y, and z. Let's give ourselves the pair (y z), any triples we like, and try to get (w x y z). Can we apply some three-cycle then (y z) to produce (w x y z)?

  • Our three-cycle should send w to x, which will be left alone by (y z).
  • It should send x to z so that when we apply (y z) it will go to y where we want it.
  • So it looks like it should be (w x z).

Now let's see what (w x z) (y z) actually does:

  • w goes to x: the first part sends it there, and the second part leaves x alone.
  • x goes to y: the first part sends it to z, and the second part then sends z to y.
  • y goes to z: the first part leaves it alone, and the second part sends it to z.
  • z goes to w: the first part sends it there, and the second part leaves w alone.

So: (w x z) (y z) = (w x y z)

If we undo the (w x z) on the left with (z x w) we get

(y z) = (z x w) (w x z) (y z) = (z x w) (w x y z)

and now we can plug in some numbers we know work, like (w x y z) = (1 2 5 4), to produce the (5 4) transposition.

Alternatively, we could just search for three-cycles that combine with A to produce a transposition. Pick three tiles below, in order, to build a triple of your choosing — it's applied the moment you've clicked a third. Then apply A on top and see what you get.

Once we have one pair, we can play the same game with pairs as we played with triples before — can you find a representative of every type of pair?

The speedrun (a digression)

Every one of the 43 quintillion configurations of a Rubik's Cube can be solved in at most 20 moves — a fact known, only half-jokingly, as God's Number. Nobody actually knew this for certain until 2010, when Tomas Rokicki and colleagues finally nailed it down using about 35 CPU-years of computing time that Google donated for the purpose.

We've shown something much easier: that every rearrangement is reachable, without considering the number of moves required. The questions "Is it possible?" and "What's the fewest moves it ever takes?" are genuinely different problems, and the second one is usually much harder. Answering it for this puzzle would not be too hard — there are only 9! = 362,880 configurations to check, but there might be some interesting related problems in larger grids.

We're done?

We haven't got a recipe, but we know that we can somehow exchange any pair of positions (and leave all the rest fixed) and that's enough to achieve any rearrangement of the positions. That deserves to be called a theorem:

Theorem. Every rearrangement of the cells of a 3×3 grid can be achieved using rotations of its 2×2 subgrids.

But wasn't the problem supposed to be about 4×4 grids? Yes — but remember the third secret (and think a little more).

Think about the 4×4 grid as four overlapping 3×3 grids. Surely we can use our power there to boost it up to the 4×4 case? In lots of cases we could just do it directly, but there are some irritating edge cases. If we want to move position 1 to the final row or final column, we can't just use the top left corner. But we can exchange 1 with any position that lies in all four grids, then exchange that position with any other. Redo the first exchange and you've swapped 1 with whatever position you like*. We can do similar things for all the edge squares. Once we can do all exchanges, we know we can do all permutations.

And why stop at 4? For 5×5 we have overlapping 4×4 grids and then the same trick. For 6×6, overlapping 5×5's etc.

Yes, we're done
